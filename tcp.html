V. Authors of Spirituality

1) Patristic Era

In the Holy Fathers are found most rich materials of the most solid Christian spirituality, but scarcely are the fundamental lines of a complete treatise on the spiritual life, as we understand it today, systematically traced. There are, however, already two syntheses much appreciated: that of Cassian, in the West, and that of St. John Climacus, in the East.

A) Greek Patrology

  • 5. St. Clement of Rome, Epistola ad Corinthios (toward 95), on concord, humility, and obedience (PG 1).
  • Hermas, Pastor (140–155) (PG 2,891–1012), in which there is extensive discussion of the return to God through penance.
  • St. Ignatius of Antioch (†c. 110), Epistulae (PG 5,62ss).
  • St. Polycarp (†146), Epistulae (PG 5,1005ss).
  • St. Clement of Alexandria, Paedagogus (after 195), in which it is shown how through ascesis one arrives at contemplation (PG 9,247–794).
  • St. Athanasius (297–373), Vita S. Antonii, in which the spirituality of the patriarch of monks and cenobites is discussed (PG 28,838–976).
  • St. Cyril of Jerusalem (315–386), Catechesis, which admirably expounds what a Christian should be (PG 33).
  • St. Basil (330–379), De Spiritu Sancto, in which the influence of the Holy Ghost in the regenerated soul is discussed (PG 32); Regulae, on monastic discipline in the East (PG 31).
  • St. Gregory of Nyssa (333–395), Comment. in Cantica canticorum and De vita Moysis, in which the contemplation and ascent of the soul to perfection is treated (PG 44,756–1120; 297–430).
  • St. Gregory Nazianzen (330–390), Sermones, particularly his prayer in praise of St. Basil (PG 35–36).
  • Evagrius Ponticus (346–99), a disciple of Origen and of the Greek monks, exercised great influence in the East, principally with his Epistulae (PG 40), De oratione and De diversis malignis cogitationibus, attributed anciently to Nilus of Ancyra (PG 79).
  • St. John Chrysostom (344–407), with his magnificent Homiliae, which constitute a treasure of moral and ascetical teaching (PG 48–64), and his precious little treatise De Sacerdotio (PG 48).
  • St. Cyril of Alexandria (†444), Thesaurus de sancta et consubstantiali Trinitate, in which the life of the soul with the Most Holy Trinity is discussed (PG 75).
  • The Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite (†c. 500), who in his books De divinis nominibus, De ecclesiastica hierarchia, and De mystica Theologia has exercised an immense influence upon all subsequent mystical theology (PG 3).
  • St. John Climacus (†649), whose Scala Paradisi is a compendium of ascetics and mystics for Eastern monks, comparable to the Collationes of Cassian for the Westerners (PG 88,632–1164).
  • Diadochus, bishop of Fotica (mid-5th c.), De perfectione spirituali capita C. (PG 65,1167–1212).
  • St. Maximus the Confessor (580–662) expounds the doctrine of Dionysius on contemplation, referring it to the incarnate Verb, who came to deify us, in his Scholia on Dionysius (PG 4), in his Librum asceticum (PG 90,912–956) and in his Mistagogia (PG 91,657–717).
  • St. John Damascene (675–749), in his three books of Sacra parallela, a most rich collection of maxims and edifying texts on the Christian life, drawn from Sacred Scripture and from the Holy Fathers, and others of lesser importance (PG 94–96).

B) Latin Patrology

  • 6. St. Cyprian (200–258), principally in De habitu virginum, De dominica oratione, De bono patientiae, De zelo et livore, De lapsis, etc. (PL 4).
  • St. Ambrose (333–397), De officiis ministrorum, De virginibus, De viduis, De virginitate, etc. (PL 16,25–302).
  • St. Jerome (347–420), Epistulae principally (PL 22).
  • St. Augustine (354–430), Confessiones, Soliloquia, De doctrina christiana, De civitate Dei, Epistulae, etc. (PL 32–47). In nearly all the works of this colossal figure are found precious ascetico-mystical materials, which all subsequent mystics have utilized.
  • Cassian (360–435), Instituta coenobiorum and Collationes (PL 49–50). His conferences or "collations" have exercised an enormous influence upon all subsequent ascetical theology.
  • St. Leo (pope, 440–461), Sermones (PL 54), full of sublime thoughts and profound piety.
  • St. Benedict (480–543), Regula (PL 66), full of gentleness and discretion, which was until the thirteenth century [the Rule] of nearly all the monks of the West.
  • St. Gregory the Great (540–604), Expositio in Librum Job, sive Moralium libri XXXV, Liber regulae pastoralis, Homiliae XL in Evangelia, Homiliae XXII in Ezechielem, Dialogorum Libri quatuor, etc. (PL 75–77).
  • St. Isidore of Seville (†636), Regula monachorum, Libri Sententiarum II–III (PL 83).

2) The Middle Ages

I. Centuries VIII to XI

This is a very poor era, which contributed scarcely anything of interest to the history of spirituality. Let us gather here something of the best that was produced in this decadent epoch:

  • St. Bede the Venerable (†735), Homiliae (PL 94).
  • Ambrose Autpert (†778), Liber de conflictu vitiorum et virtutum, attributed anciently to St. Ambrose or to St. Augustine (PL 40,1091).
  • St. Theodore the Studite (759–826), Catechesis minor et maior and Sermones de sanctis monachis (PG 99).
  • John Aurelianensis (†843); three books De institutione laicali, a manual of piety for laypeople (PL 106).
  • Smaragdus (†c. 830), Commentarium in Regulam S. Benedicti, Diadema monachorum (PL 102).
  • Odo of Cluny (†942), Collationes (PL 133).
  • St. Peter Damian (†1072), Epistulae and Sermones (PL 145).
  • Simeon the Theologian (949–1022), Sermones, Hymni mystici, etc. (PG 120).

II. Centuries XII to XV

The distinct schools of spirituality begin to take shape around the great religious orders. The teachings of the Holy Fathers are organized and systematized with the new contributions of theologians and experimental mystics.

A) Benedictine School

It is inspired principally in the liturgy and in the monastic observances through the Rule of its holy Founder. Here are the principal representatives:

  • St. Anselm (1033–1109), principally in his most devout Meditationes et orationes (PL 158, with some interpolations of other authors) and Cur Deus homo, in which the gravity of sin and the infinite satisfaction of Christ are discussed (PL 158).
  • St. Bernard (1090–1153), the Doctor Mellifluus, whose intimate devotion and tenderness has had a deep repercussion upon all subsequent spirituality. Cf. principally: De consideratione (to Pope Eugene III), De diligendo Deo, De gradibus humilitatis, De conversione ad clericos…, Sermones, In Cantica canticorum, Epistulae, etc. (PL 182–84). A good study on St. Bernard is that of Étienne Gilson, La Théologie mystique de S. Bernard (1934).
  • St. Hildegard (1098–1179) in her Scivias (sci vias Domini), Liber operum Domini, Epistulae, etc. (PL 197).
  • St. Gertrude the Great (1256–1301) and St. Matilde of Hackeborn (c. 1242–1299) have some most notable Revelationes, in which—among other very interesting things—a tender devotion to the Sacred Heart of Jesus is manifested.
  • St. Bridget (1302–1373), a widow, a nun in the Cistercian monastery of Alvastra (Sweden); she has some famous Revelationes which describe particularly the Passion of the Lord.
  • John of Kastl (†c. 1410), probable author of the precious De adhaerendo Deo, attributed until recently to St. Albert the Great; other writings of his: De lumine increato, Formulae vitae religiosae, etc., remain still unpublished.
  • Ludovico Barbo (1380–1443) composed his Formula orationis et meditationis, in which the current of methodical prayer that St. Ignatius would perfect is initiated.
B) School of Saint Victor

Heir to the spirit of St. Augustine and to the doctrines of the Pseudo-Dionysius, the school of Saint Victor—founded by William of Champeaux—has Platonic and allegorist tendencies. It represents a middle term between the Benedictine school, of a predominantly affective orientation, and the Dominican, which will be born next with a more intellectualist tendency. Its principal representatives are:

  • Hugh of Saint Victor (1096–1141) in his Comment. in Hierarchiam caelest. Ps.-Dionysii (PL 175), De vanitate mundi, Expositio in Regulam S. Augustini, De Institutione novitiorum, De arrha animae, De laude caritatis, De modo orandi, De meditando (PL 176).
  • Richard of Saint Victor (†1173), De praeparatione ad contemplationem (Beniamin minor), De gratia contemplationis (Beniamin maior), De statu interioris hominis, De eruditione interioris hominis, De gradibus caritatis, De IV gradibus violentae caritatis, Expositio in Cantica canticorum, etc. (PL 196).
  • Adam (†1177) is the poet of the school with his Sequentiae (PL 196).
C) Carthusian School

Following the spirit of its Order, the Carthusian school insists upon the solitary and contemplative life. Its principal representatives up to the eighteenth century are:

  • Guido I (†1137) was the fifth prior and true legislator of the Charterhouse: Consuetudines carthusienses (PL 153), Meditationes (ed. Paris 1936).
  • Guido II (†c. 1193), also prior: Scala Claustralium (lectio, meditation, prayer, and contemplation) (PL 184–475).
  • Hugo of Balma (13th c.) is, it seems, the true author of the famous Theologia mystica, or better, De triplici via ad sapientiam, attributed for several centuries to St. Bonaventure.
  • Ludolph of Saxony (†1377), who was first a Dominican and later entered the Charterhouse, is the author of the famous Vita Christi (known as the Carthusian), which so greatly influenced subsequent spirituality and did much good for St. Teresa (1st ed. 1477).
  • Denis the Carthusian (1402–1471) was called the Doctor Ecstaticus; he wrote many books (44 vols. in 4° in the new edition begun in 1896 by the Carthusians of Montreuil), among others his Commentaries on Cassian, on St. John Climacus, on the Pseudo-Dionysius, De arcta via salutis et contemptu mundi, De conversione peccatoris, De remediis contra tentationes, Speculum conversionis peccatorum, De oratione, De fonte lucis, De contemplatione, De discretione spirituum, De donis Spiritus Sancti, etc. (ed. Monstrolli 1896–1923).
  • John Lanspergius (†1539), celebrated for his devotion to the Sacred Heart of Jesus and for his principal work Alloquium Christi ad animam fidelem, which closely resembles the Imitatio Christi (ed. Tornaci).
  • Lorenzo Surio (†1578) published six volumes De probatis Sanctorum historiis, perfecting the work of A. Lippomani, and translated into Latin the sermons of Tauler.
  • Antonio de Molina (†1619), Instrucción de los sacerdotes (1608), a precious book of which there have been numerous editions and translations; Ejercicios espirituales, De las excelencias, provecho y necesidad de la oración mental (Burgos 1615).
  • Inocencio Le Masson (†1703), Introduction à la vie religieuse et parfaite (1677), Disciplina Ordinis Carthusiensis (1703), Psalmodie intérieure (4 vols., 1696–97).
  • Agustín Nagore (1620–1705), of the Charterhouse of Zaragoza (Aula Dei), published his famous Lucerna mystica (Valencia 1690) under the pseudonym of José López Ezquerra, Presb. In it he opposes the errors of Molinos, the same as in his Lydius theologicus.
D) Dominican School

Upon a solid doctrinal base it joins liturgical prayer and contemplation with apostolic action. St. Dominic was a living incarnation of the motto of the Order: Contemplata aliis tradere.

  • St. Dominic de Guzmán (1170–1221), founder of the Order of Preachers, composed his Constitutiones (Liber consuetudinum)—inspired by those of the Premonstratensians—which are most apt for harmonizing the contemplative life with the active, which is the ideal of his Order.
  • Jordan of Saxony (†1237), first successor of St. Dominic, wrote some notable Epistulae spirituales (ed. Altaner, Leipzig 1925).
  • Humbert of Romans (†1277), fifth master general, commented upon the Rule and the Constitutions with much doctrine, piety, and unction: Expositio super Regulam Sancti Augustini et Constitutiones Fr. Praedicatorum.
  • Hugh of Saint-Cher (†1263): with his magnificent commentaries on Sacred Scripture one has been able to form an excellent book, De vita spirituali (P. Dionisio Mésard, O.P.; Pustet 1910), divided into four parts: purgative, illuminative, unitive, and the spiritual life of priests.
  • St. Albert the Great (†1280), the great master of Thomas Aquinas, wrote an astonishing number of books on the most diverse subjects (38 volumes in 4° of the Vivès ed., 1890–99), of which there are of interest here above all his Commentaries on Dionysius the Areopagite, Super Ioannem, his precious Mariale, De sancto sacrificio missae, Summa theologiae, Commentarii in Sententias, etc.
  • St. Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274), the Doctor Angelicus, is indisputably the first authority in ascetical and mystical Theology (Pius XI) and the master of all subsequent theologians. His ecumenical work and his objective doctrine surpass the molds of any particular school so as to make him the Common and Universal Doctor, as the Church calls him. In his works are found, scattered, the great principles of the Theology of the spiritual life and all the fundamental questions of Christian ascetics and mystics. See principally his marvelous Summa Theologiae, his Commentaries on St. Paul, on the Gospels, on the Pseudo-Dionysius; his opuscule De perfectione vitae spiritualis and his Officium Sanctissimi Sacramenti, overflowing with doctrine and piety.
  • Meister Eckhart (c. 1260–1327) wrote many ascetico-mystical works, but scarcely any have come down to us save a few fragments, for after the condemnation of some of his propositions by John XXII—after the death of Eckart—his complete works were destroyed (cf. Denz. 501–529). He exercised a great influence upon his disciples Tauler and Suso.
  • Johannes Tauler (†1361) is one of the greatest mystics of the world, who exercised a great influence upon subsequent mystics, above all upon St. John of the Cross (cf. P. Crisógono, San Juan de la Cruz su obra científica, intr., p. 45). He expounded his doctrine in his Sermones, which had a great diffusion through the translation of Surio. The famous Institutiones divinae were not written by him, but contain a summary of his doctrine (ed. P. Getino, Madrid 1922).
  • Bl. Henry Suso (c. 1295–1366): Horologium Sapientiae, Libellus de Veritate, Sermones, etc. His works were published in German by P. Denifle, Die Schriften des heiligen H. Suso, and in French by P. Thiriot, Œuvres mystiques de H. Suso (Gabalda, Paris 1899).
  • St. Catherine of Siena (1347–1380): her famous work Dialogo and her precious Lettere constitute a most rich arsenal of ascetico-mystical doctrines of the first quality.
  • St. Vincent Ferrer (1346–1419) has several ascetical opuscules, above all his famous Tractatus de vita spirituali, which circulated in manuscript form for several centuries.
  • St. Antoninus of Florence (†1459), in his opuscule Regola di vita christiana, gives excellent norms of sanctification (ed. Florence 1923).
  • Girolamo Savonarola (†1498) wrote his books Trattato dell'umiltà, Trattato dell'ordine, Trattato dell'amore di Gesù Cristo, Trattato della semplicità della vita cristiana, his precious commentaries Esposizione del Pater Noster and Esposizione del Miserere, etc., apart from his most notable Prediche.
  • Domingo Cavalca (†1342), Specchio della croce, Disciplina delli spirituali, etc.
  • Jacobo Passavanti (†1357), Specchio di vera penitenza (ed. Florence 1924).
  • Giovanni Dominici (†1419), Libro d'amore di carità, Regola del governo di cura familiari (ed. Salvi, 1860).
  • Card. Juan of Torquemada (†1468), wrote Meditationes de vita Christi (1467), Quaestiones spirituales super Evangelia (1478), and De nuptiis spiritualibus (unpublished).
  • Bl. Osana of Mantua (†1505), Liber vitae suae et donorum spiritualium a Deo acceptorum.
E) Franciscan School

It insists above all upon the doctrine of love and upon the necessity of one's own abnegation and of perfect poverty in order to imitate Christ.

  • St. Francis of Assisi (1181–1226), Opuscula (ed. critica, Quaracchi 1904), Opera omnia (ed. Fonti Francescane).
  • St. Anthony of Padua (†1231) has many things of mystics in his Sermones dominicales et in solemnitatibus (ed. Locatelli, Padua 1895–1903).
  • David of Augsburg (†1271) was much read in his De exterioris et interioris hominis reformatione (ed. Quaracchi 1899) and in De septem gradibus orationis.
  • St. Bonaventure (1221–1274): besides his theological works, he has many ascetico-mystical treatises, among which the famous Itinerarium mentis in Deum, the Breviloquium, the Incendium amoris (called also De triplici via), the Lignum vitae, Vitis mystica, De sex alis Seraphim, etc., stand out. He exercised great influence upon all subsequent mystics, above all upon his school.
  • Bl. Raymond Lull (†1315) is an exalted mystic in his Liber de amico et amato, which forms part of his Blanquerna (ed. critica, Palma de Mallorca 1914).
  • St. Bernardino of Siena (1380–1444), Sermones, Scripta ascetica (ed. Roma 1903).
  • Hendrik Herp (†1477): his Theologia mystica was placed at the beginning in the Index; but, corrected by the Dominican P. Philip (Roma 1586), it exercised great influence in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.
  • St. Angela of Foligno (†1309): the famous Liber visionum et instructionum consists of a Memoriale, gathered by her confessor, and of other documents. It describes principally the sovereign transcendence of God and the torments of Jesus Christ.
  • St. Catherine of Bologna (1413–1463) in her Revelations, with the title De septem armis spiritualibus, gives excellent practical means for overcoming temptations.
F) Independent Authors

We group here some of the most distinguished mystical writers who have exercised influence upon Christian spirituality, but without it being possible to place them in any particular school. The principal ones are:

  • Bl. Jan van Ruusbroec (1293–1381), called the Admirable, is the founder of the so-called Flemish mystical school, which has him as its principal and almost sole representative. His exalted mysticism exercised an enormous influence upon subsequent centuries. His principal works are Spiegel der eeuwigher salicheit, Boec van den seven sloten, Die geestelike brulocht, Dat rijcke der ghelieven, Dat boecxken der verclaringhe (often called Dat boecxken der blinkender steen, "The Little Book of the Sparkling Stone"), Van den seven trappen der minnen, etc. (Original text, ed. crítica Ruysbroeck Genootschap Mechelen, 4 vols. [1932–34]; Latin translation of Surio [1552]).
  • Gerardo Groot (1340–1384) is the author of diverse ascetical opuscules.
  • Gerlac Peters (1378–1411): Soliloquium ignitum, of doctrine akin to that of the Imitation (ed. Rotterdam 1936).
  • Thomas à Kempis (1379–1471), to whom is attributed the marvelous De imitatione Christi, the spiritual book most widely read in the world. He also wrote Orationes et meditationes de vita Christi, Soliloquium animae, De tribus tabernaculis, De vera compunctione, De disciplina claustralium, etc. (ed. critica, 7 vols., Freiburg 1902–22).
  • Johannes Mauburnus (†1503) summarized the principal questions of spirituality in his Rosetum exercitiorum spiritualium (1491).
  • Card. Pierre d'Ailly (1350–1420), Sermones and Tractatus (De quatuor gradibus scholae spiritualis, Speculum considerationis, Compendium contemplationis, etc.), De falsis prophetis (among the works of Jean Gerson, t. 1).
  • St. Lawrence Justinian (1318–1455), reformer of the Italian congregations and of the secular clergy, wrote many ascetico-mystical works, among which stand out: De spirituali et casto connubio Verbi et animae, De perfectionis gradibus, De compunctione, De humilitate, De obedientia, De contemptu mundi, De incendio divini amoris, etc. (Opera omnia, Venice 1751).
  • Walter Hilton (†1396) wrote his famous The Scale of Perfection, which has earned him the title of head of the English school.
  • Jean Gerson (1363–1429): he erred profoundly in some nominalist theses (nullus est actus intrinsece malus ex obiecto), but he wrote excellent spiritual works: Liber de vita spirituali animae, Mons contemplationis, Theologia mystica speculativa et practica, De probatione spirituum, De oratione, De passionibus animae, De tentationibus, De perfectione cordis, etc. His are also the precious little treatise De parvulis ad Christum trahendis and some devout Considerationes de Sancto Ioseph, which place him among the first and most decided promoters of devotion to Joseph (Opera, ed. Dupin, 1706).
  • Juliana of Norwich (1342–†1413?), Revelations of divine Love, new ed., London 1907.
  • St. Catherine of Genoa (1447–1510): Dialogo d'amore divino (of which only the first part is authentic) and the precious Trattato del purgatorio.

3) Modern and Contemporary Age

Centuries XVI to XX

The ancient schools—Benedictine, Dominican, and Franciscan—continue spreading and making concrete their doctrines. New schools arise, such as the Carmelite, the Augustinian, the Ignatian, and the French school of the seventeenth century. Mysticism is codified and systematized, making it more organic and scientific. The discussions among schools begin. The mystical heresies are combated.

A) Benedictine School
  • García de Cisneros (1455–1510), abbot of Montserrat, composed his Exercitatorio de la vida espiritual, which perhaps inspired St. Ignatius with the first idea of his Exercitia spiritualia (ed. Barcelona 1912).
  • Ludovico Blosio (1506–1566) stands out above all for his precious Institutio vitae spiritualis, in which he gathers all his doctrines. He also wrote Consolatio pusillanimium, Conclave animae fidelis, etc., and a defense of Johannes Tauler, explaining him in a more accessible style (Opera omnia, 1632).
  • Juan de Castañiza (†1598) continues in Spain the reform of García de Cisneros, and wrote De perfectione vitae christianae and the five books Institutionum divinae pietatis.
  • Augustine Baker (1575–1641) wrote several opuscules on contemplation, collected later under the title Sancta Sophia by Serenus Cressy (1657).
  • Armand Jean le Bouthillier de Rancé (†1700), reformer of the Cistercians (Trappists), wrote De la sainteté et des devoirs de la vie monastique (1683) and his Responsio ad tractatum de studiis monasticis (1692), against Jean Mabillon, who conceded too much importance to literary studies in the monastic life.
  • Card. Giovanni Bona (1609–1674) is famous, above all, for his excellent treatise De discretione spirituum. He also wrote De divina psalmodia, Via compendii ad Deum, Manuductio ad caelum, Horologium asceticum, etc.
  • Dominic Schram (1658–1720), Institutiones theologiae mysticae; it is an excellent treatise on ascetics and mystics, didactic and pious at the same time (new edition, Paris 1868).
  • Dom Prosper Guéranger (1805–1875), restorer of the Benedictine Order in France, wrote the first nine volumes of his monumental L'Année liturgique, which has done so much good for souls. He also wrote his Conférences sur la vie chrétienne (1880).
  • Cecilia Bruyère (†1909), abbess of St. Cecilia, wrote La vie spirituelle et l'oraison d'après la Sainte Écriture et la tradition monastique (new ed. 1922).
  • Dom Vital Lehodey, Cistercian abbot of Notre-Dame de Grâce, stands out for his precious works Les voies de l'oraison mentale (1908) and Le saint abandon (1919).
  • Dom Cuthbert Butler (1858–1934) has written Benedictine monachism (1919), Western Mysticism (1922), Ways of christian life (London 1932).
  • Dom Savinien Louismet (1858–1926) has written several works on mystical prayer: Essai sur la connaissance mystique, La vie mystique, Miracle et mystique, La contemplation chrétienne, etc.
  • Bl. Columba Marmion (1858–1923), illustrious abbot of Maredsous, is perhaps the most famous contemporary mystical author of the world. His influence has been enormous through his precious works: Christus, vita animae (1918), Christus in mysteriis suis (1919), Christus, idealis monachi (1922), Sponsa Verbi (1923), and De unione cum Deo (extracts of letters, 1934).
  • Dom Jean-Baptiste Chautard (†1936) is the author of the precious little work L'âme de tout apostolat, very well known throughout the world.
  • Dom André Malet has written in compendious form a notable work, La vie surnaturelle: ses éléments, son exercice (Paris 1934).
  • Dom Anselm Stolz (†1942), Theologia mystica, is an original and notable contribution to mystical studies.
  • Dom Germain Morin, L'idéal monastique et la vie chrétienne des premiers siècles.
B) Dominican School
  • Bautista de Crema (†1534): Via di aperta verità, Specchio interiore, Della cognitione et vittoria di se stesso (1531), which was adapted into Spanish by Melchor Cano, etc.
  • St. Catherine of Ricci (1522–1590), Lettere (ed. Firenze 1890).
  • Pablo de León (†1528), Guía del cielo (Alcalá 1553).
  • Melchor Cano (†1560), La victoria de sí mismo (Valladolid, 1550), adaptation of the work of Crema.
  • Juan de la Cruz (†c. 1565), Diálogo sobre la necesidad de la oración (Salamanca 1555).
  • Felipe de Meneses (†1572), Luz del alma (Valladolid 1554).
  • Agustín de Esbarroya (†1554), Purificador de la conciencia (Sevilla 1550).
  • Domingo Baltanás (†1564), Apología de la oración mental (Sevilla 1556), Apología de la frecuentación de la sacrosanta Eucaristía y Comunión (Sevilla 1558), Doctrina cristiana (Sevilla 1555).
  • Alonso Cabrera (1548–1598), Los escrúpulos y sus remedios (Valencia 1599; reed. P. Getino, Madrid 1918).
  • Ven. Luis de Granada (1505–1588) is the Spanish author who has attained the greatest number of editions in the entire world. His precious works—Guía de pecadores, De la oración y meditación, Memorial de la vida cristiana, Exposición del símbolo de la fe, etc.—have not lost their freshness and currency, being for souls an inexhaustible wellspring of the purest and most refined devotion.
  • St. Bartolomé de los Mártires (1514–1590), archbishop of Braga, Compendium spiritualis doctrinae (1582), Stimulus pastorum (1564).
  • Juan Gavaston (†1623), El tratado de la Vida espiritual de San Vicente Ferrer declarado y comentado (Valencia 1626).
  • John of St. Thomas (1589–1644) is the best commentator of the Angelic Doctor on the question relative to the gifts of the Holy Ghost (In I-II, q. 68).
  • Louis Chardon (1595–1651) wrote his magnificent works La Croix de Jésus (1647; reed. Paris 1937), Raccourci de l'art de méditer (1649), Méditations sur la Passion (1650).
  • Thomas de Vallgornera (†1665) collected innumerable texts of St. Thomas in his Mystica Theologia Divi Thomae (ed. Turín 1911), inspiring himself much in the works of the Carmelite Philippus a Sanctissima Trinitate.
  • Juan Tomás de Rocabert (†1699), Exercitium meditationum (Barcelona 1668), Theologia mystica (ibid., 1669).
  • Vincent Contenson (1641–1674) is the author of the famous Theologia mentis et cordis, in 9 vols.
  • Bl. Francisco de Posadas (†1713), Triunfos de la castidad, against the quietist errors of Molinos.
  • Antoine Massoulié (1632–1706), Traité de la véritable oraison (1699), Traité de l'amour de Dieu (1703), against quietism; Méditations de Saint Thomas sur les trois voies (ed. Paris 1934).
  • Domenico Ricci, Homo interior (3 vols., 1709), against the errors of Molinos.
  • Alexandre Piny (1640–1709), L'abandon à la Providence divine, La prière du cœur, La clef du pur amour, L'état du pur amour, La présence de Dieu, etc.
  • J. B. Rousseau, Avis sur les divers états d'oraison (1710).
  • Henri-Dominique Lacordaire (1802–1861), Vie de saint Dominique (1840), Lettres.
  • André Meynard (1824–1904), Traité de la vie intérieure (1884), new edition adapted by P. Gerest (1923–25).
  • B. Froget, De l'habitation du Saint-Esprit dans les âmes justes (1900).
  • M. J. Rousset, Directorium asceticum, La vie spirituelle d'après la tradition et la doctrine des Saints (1902).
  • Bl. Hyacinthe-Marie Cormier (1832–1916), Instruction des novices (1905), Retraites, Lettres, etc.
  • Heinrich Denifle (†1908), Das geistliche Leben (collection of texts of the German mystics of the fourteenth century).
  • Ambroise Gardeil (1859–1931), La structure de l'âme et l'expérience mystique (magnificent work in 2 vols., Paris 1927), La vraie vie chrétienne (incomplete, Paris 1935), De donis Spiritus Sancti in sanctis dominicanis.
  • Barthier, De la perfection chrétienne et religieuse d'après S. Thomas et S. François de Sales (2 vols., 1902).
  • M. A. Janvier (1860–1939), Exposition de la Morale Catholique, conferences at Notre Dame of Paris (from 1903 to 1923), principally the conferences on charity and Christian perfection.
  • Juan González Arintero (1860–1928) is the restorer of mystical studies in Spain. His works—Evolución mística, Cuestiones místicas, Exposición del Cantar de los Cantares, La verdadera mística tradicional, Grados de oración, Las escalas de amor, etc.—are reedited ceaselessly. In 1921 he founded in Salamanca La vida sobrenatural.
  • Reginald Garrigou-Lagrange is one of the most important figures of contemporary mystics: Perfection chrétienne et contemplation (2 vols., 1923), L'amour de Dieu et la Croix de Jésus (2 vols., 1929), La Providence et la confiance en Dieu, Les trois âges de la vie intérieure, Les trois conversions et les trois voies (Juvisy 1933), L'union mystique en sainte Catherine de Sienne (1938), Le Sauveur et son amour pour nous, La Mère du Sauveur et notre vie intérieure.
  • M. M. Philipon, La doctrine spirituelle de Sœur Élisabeth de la Trinité (1937), a truly outstanding work; Sainte Thérèse de Lisieux (Barcelona 1952), Les sacrements dans la vie chrétienne, La doctrine spirituelle de Dom Marmion (Paris 1954), and Les dons du Saint-Esprit (1966).
  • Ignacio G. Menéndez-Reigada (†1951), Unidad específica de la contemplación cristiana (Madrid 1926), De dirección espiritual (Salamanca 1934), Los dones del Espíritu Santo y la perfección cristiana (Madrid 1948).
  • Emilio Sauras, El Cuerpo místico de Cristo (BAC, Madrid 1952).
  • Paul Philippe, Doctrina mystica S. Thomae (Roma 1952).
C) Franciscan School
  • Alonso de Madrid (†1521) wrote his precious Arte para servir a Dios, a true gem of our ascetico-mystical literature, and the Espejo de ilustres personas (ed. BAC, n. 38).
  • Francisco de Osuna (†c. 1540), Primero, segundo y tercer Abecedario espiritual (1525–1530). The third, which St. Teresa used much; Ley de amor santo (BAC, n. 38), which constitutes his fourth Abecedario, a summary of the preceding ones.
  • Bernardino de Laredo (1482–1540), a lay religious, wrote his celebrated Subida del monte Sión, full of light, sweetness, and coloring (BAC, n. 44).
  • Antonio de Guevara (1480–1545), Monte Calvario and Oratorio de religiosos y ejercicios virtuosos (BAC, n. 44).
  • St. Peter of Alcántara (1499–1562), Tratado de la oración, a brief summary of the book De la Oración y meditación, by Fr. Luis de Granada (new edition, Salamanca 1926).
  • Juan de Bonilla, Tratado de la paz del alma (Alcalá 1580).
  • Bl. Nicolás Factor (1520–1583), Las tres vías, a brief little treatise of exalted mysticism, full of allegories (BAC, n. 44).
  • Diego de Estella (1524–1578) wrote the famous treatise De la Vanidad del mundo and his Meditaciones devotísimas del amor de Dios (BAC, n. 46).
  • Friar Juan de los Ángeles (1536–1609) is one of the greatest Franciscan mystics in his Triunfos del amor de Dios, Diálogos de la conquista del reino de Dios, Consideraciones sobre los Cantares, Manual de la vida perfecta (BAC, n. 46), etc.
  • Benoît de Canfield (†1610), O.M.Cap., Règle de perfection (1609).
  • Ven. Mary of Ágreda (1602–1665), Mística ciudad de Dios, Escala para subir a la perfección, Leyes de la esposa (new ed. Barcelona 1911–1920, 7 vols.).
  • Card. Brancati de Laurea (†1693), De oratione christiana (1685), much cited by Benedict XIV.
  • St. Veronica Giuliani (1660–1727), Diario (9 vols., Prato 1895–1928).
  • Ambroise de Lombez (†1778), Traité de la paix intérieure, very efficacious against scruples (numerous editions); Lettres spirituelles (3 vols., Paris 1881–1882).
  • Card. Vives y Tutó, Compendium theologiae ascetico-mysticae (1908).
  • J. Heerinckx, Introductio in theologiam spiritualem (Roma 1931).
  • Yves de Mohon, Le don de Sagesse (Paris 1928).
D) Augustinian School

It is inspired by preference in some of its own authors of the Middle Ages (Ruysbroeck, Kempis, etc.), but it does not seem well defined until the Modern Age. Here are some of its most notable authors:

  • St. Thomas of Villanova (1488–1555), besides his magnificent Sermones, wrote several ascetical opuscules (Obras, BAC, n. 96).
  • St. Alonso de Orozco (1500–1591) has greater importance than the preceding in the history of mystics; Vergel de oración y monte de contemplación, Desposorio espiritual, Libro de la suavidad de Dios and Regla de vida cristiana are his principal works (Madrid 1736, 4 vols.).
  • Ven. Tomé de Jesús (1533–1582) wrote, among others, his most beautiful work Os trabalhos de Jesus, hardly surpassable (various editions).
  • Fray Luis de León (†1591), De los nombres de Cristo, La perfecta casada, Exposición del Cantar de los Cantares, Del libro de Job, etc. (Obras, BAC, n. 3).
  • Pedro Malón de Chaide (1530–1589), La conversión de la Magdalena, of exuberant coloring and marvelous style, but of mystics somewhat artificial (various editions).
  • Agustín Antolínez (1554–1626) commented with much piety the mystical poems of St. John of the Cross in his famous Exposición.
  • Enrique Flórez (1702–1773), the immortal author of the España Sagrada, also wrote some works of mystics, such as El Libro de los libros y ciencia de los santos, Modo práctico de tener oración mental and his precious translation of Os trabalhos de Jesus.
  • César Vaca, Guías de almas (3rd ed.); Haz meditación (2nd ed.); La vida religiosa en San Agustín (2 vols.).
  • For a more abundant Augustinian bibliography, see P. Monasterio, Místicos agustinos españoles (El Escorial 1929, 2 vols.).
E) The Carmelite School

Although it has some historical antecedents, the school properly does not appear until the sixteenth century with St. Teresa and St. John of the Cross. It is a highly contemplative spirituality, in which everything is ordered to intimate union with God through the detachment from all created things, recollection, and the life of continual prayer.

  • St. Teresa of Jesus (1515–1582) is, without dispute, the summit figure of the experimental Christian mystics. In her works is found the highest psychology of mystical prayer that the centuries have bequeathed to us. Her admirable descriptions, her marvelous classification of the degrees of prayer, and a hundred other mystical phenomena have not been surpassed by anyone. Her immortal works, translated into the principal languages of the world, are reedited ceaselessly and are solid and exquisite food for thirsting souls (Obras, ed. crítica del P. Silverio [9 vols., Burgos 1915–1926]).
  • St. John of the Cross (1542–1591) marks, with St. Teresa, the culminating moment of the experimental Christian mystics, with the advantage over the Saint that the Mystical Doctor does not limit himself to expounding phenomena, but gives the ratio of them, illuminating them with the great principles of the philosophy and Catholic theology (Obras, ed. crítica P. Silverio [5 vols., Burgos 1929–1931]; ed. manual BAC, n. 15).
  • Juan de Jesús María Aravalles (1539–1609), Instrucción de novicios (reed., Toledo 1925) and Tratado de la oración (Toledo 1926).
  • Jerónimo Gracián de la Madre de Dios (1545–1614), great friend and disciple of St. Teresa, wrote his Dilucidario del verdadero espíritu (1604), Mística teología (1601), Vida del alma (1609), etc. (Obras, ed. P. Silverio, 3 vols., Burgos 1932–1933).
  • St. María Maddalena de Pazzi (1566–1607), Carmelite of the Ancient Observance: Estasi e lettere scelte (ed. crítica, Firenze 1924).
  • Tomás de Jesús (1564–1627), Tratado de la oración mental (1610), De contemplatione divina (1620), De contemplatione acquisita (Milan 1922). He was the one who introduced in his school the formula "acquired contemplation," which caused so much disorientation in mystical studies.
  • Nicolás de Jesús María (†c. 1670?), Elucidatio Theologica (Alcalá 1631), in defense of the doctrine of St. John of the Cross.
  • Philip of the Most Holy Trinity (†1671), Summa theologiae mysticae in 3 vols. (1656; reed. Paris 1875), in which the Dominican Tomás de Vallgornera drew much inspiration.
  • José del Espíritu Santo (†1736), an Andalusian, wrote his monumental Cursus theologiae mystico-scholasticae, in 6 vols. (new ed. Bruges, t. I–4, 1924ss).
  • Bl. Thérèse of the Child Jesus (1873–1897), in her Histoire d'une âme, Lettres, Poésies, etc., has made herself most famous throughout the world for her "little way" of spiritual infancy.
  • St. Elizabeth of the Trinity (1880–1906), in her Souvenirs and Retraites, shows herself to be one of the most sublime souls that have passed through the Carmelite cloisters.
  • Crisógono de Jesús Sacramentado (1904–1945), San Juan de la Cruz: su obra científica y literaria (2 vols., Ávila 1929), La escuela mística carmelitana (Ávila 1930), Vida de San Juan de la Cruz (BAC, n. 15), Compendio de ascética y mística (Ávila 1933).
  • Marie-Eugène de l'Enfant-Jésus, Je veux voir Dieu.
F) Ignatian School

An active, energetic, practical, disciplined, methodized spirituality. Everything is foreseen with mathematical precision. Through its robust frame, the spirit of the iron-willed Basque who founded it is transparently seen. It tends to form the will for personal sanctification and the apostolate.

  • St. Ignatius of Loyola (c. 1491–1495, †1556), founder of the Company of Jesus and of its name through his most famous Ejercicios Espirituales, which have exercised a great influence upon all subsequent spirituality. To know his spirit one must also read his Autobiografía, his Diario espiritual, the Constituciones de la Compañía de Jesús and his magnificent Cartas (Obras completas, ed. BAC, n. 86).
  • Luis de la Puente (†1624), Meditaciones (1605), Guía espiritual (1609), perhaps the best work of the properly mystical school; De la perfección del cristiano en todos sus estados (1612).
  • Louis Lallemant (†1635), Doctrine spirituelle, gathered by his disciple Rigoleuc (reed. 1924 and 1936); a precious book, the best that this school has produced.
  • Jean-Joseph Surin (†1665), Fondements de la vie spirituelle (1674; reed. Paris 1930), Questions sur l'amour de Dieu (ed. Paris 1930), Lettres spirituelles (ed. Toulouse 1926).
  • Jean-Pierre de Caussade (†1751), Instructions spirituelles sur les divers états d'oraison, L'abandon à la Providence divine (various editions).
  • Jean-Nicolas Grou (1731–1803), Caractères de la vraie dévotion (1778), Maximes spirituelles (1789), Manuel des âmes intérieures (1803), L'Intérieur de Jésus et de Marie (1815). He is one of the most solid and reliable authors of the school.
  • Auguste Poulain (†1918), Des grâces d'oraison (1901), a very useful and celebrated work (11 ed., Paris 1931), but it gathers exclusively the psychological aspect of mystics, omitting the theological entirely.
  • Joseph Maréchal (†1941), Études sur la psychologie des mystiques (I, 1924; II, 1937).
  • Joseph de Guibert (1877–1942), Études de théologie mystique (1930), Theologia spiritualis ascetica et mystica (ed. Roma 1939), Documenta ecclesiastica christianae perfectionis studium spectantia (Roma 1931).
  • The Fathers of the Company—with the collaboration of many other religious, priests, and lay people—have been publishing for some years a most notable Dictionnaire de Spiritualité (Beauchesne, Paris), which constitutes a precious and indispensable instrument of work.
G) Salesian School

Properly speaking, it does not constitute a school apart, being subsidiary of various related schools, above all the French of the seventeenth century. But we group under this name the principal authors who recognize St. Francis de Sales as their leader and follow the footsteps of his sweet and charming spirituality.

  • St. Francis de Sales (1567–1622) is one of the authors who most profoundly influenced subsequent spirituality through his Introduction à la vie dévote (1609), his Lettres and Les Vrais Entretiens spirituels, and, above all, his magnificent Traité de l'amour de Dieu (1616). See Oeuvres complètes (Annecy 1892ss).
  • Joseph Tissot (†1894), La Vie intérieure simplifiée et réduite à son unique principe, a precious work written by a Carthusian, L'art de profiter de ses fautes d'après Saint François de Sales (1878).
  • F. Vincent, Saint François de Sales, directeur d'âmes (1923).
H) French School of the Seventeenth Century

A most rich spirituality, founded upon the dogma of our incorporation into Christ, the incarnate Verb, by the sacrament of baptism. Temples of the Holy Ghost by baptismal grace, we must, in union with the incarnate Verb, glorify God, who lives in us, and copy the interior virtues of Jesus, destroying completely the old man: "For let this mind be in you, which was also in Christ Jesus" (Phil 2:5); "…stripping yourselves of the old man… and putting on the new" (Col 3:9–10).

  • Card. Pierre de Bérulle (1575–1629) is the founder of the school and of the Congregation of the Oratory in France. His principal work is his Discours de l'état et des grandeurs de Jésus (1623), but he has also Traité de l'abnégation and many other opuscules and letters (Oeuvres complètes, reed. Migne 1856).
  • Jean-Jacques Olier (1608–1657), founder of the Company of St. Sulpice and one of the best expositors of the spirituality of the French school in his Catéchisme chrétien pour la vie intérieure (1656), Introduction à la vie et aux vertus chrétiennes (1647), Traité de SS. Ordres (1676), Journée chrétienne, Lettres, etc.
  • St. John Eudes (1601–1680), founder of the Congregation of Jesus and Mary (Eudists), is an incessant promoter of the devotion to the Sacred Hearts. His principal works are: La vie et le royaume de Jésus dans les âmes chrétiennes (1637), Le Coeur admirable de la Mère de Dieu (1681), etc. (Oeuvres, 12 vols., Vannes 1905ss).
  • St. Louis-Marie Grignion de Montfort (1673–1716), a member of the Company of Mary, stands out in his works principally for the Marian aspect of the school.
  • St. John Baptist de la Salle (1651–1719), founder of the Brothers of the Christian Schools: Explication de la méthode d'oraison (1739), Méditations pour le temps de la retraite (c. 1725), etc.
  • Jérôme Ribet (1873–1909), La mystique divine distinguée des contrefaçons diaboliques et des analogies humaines (4 vols., Paris 1871–83), L'ascétique chrétienne (Paris 1887).
  • A. Tanquerey (1854–1932), Précis de théologie ascétique et mystique (1923–24).
I) Liguorian School

No such school exists with specific proper characteristics. But we group under this title St. Alphonsus and his principal disciples. They insist above all upon the love of God, upon prayer, and upon mortification.

  • St. Alphonsus Maria de Liguori (1696–1787), besides being a great moralist, is the author of numerous and excellent ascetical works: Visite al Santissimo Sacramento e a Maria Santissima (1745), Le glorie di Maria (1759), Del gran mezzo della preghiera (1750), La vera sposa di Gesù Cristo (1760), Pratica di amar Gesù Cristo (1768), etc.
  • Gennaro Sarnelli (1702–1744), Il mondo santificato, La discrezione degli spiriti, Lettere spirituali, etc. (Opere, Naples 1877).
  • Joseph Schrijvers (1876–1945), Les principes de la vie spirituelle (Paris 1913), Le don de soi, Le divin Ami, etc.
J) Independent Authors

We continue under this heading the list of authors from the secular clergy or of those others who by their ideas cannot be placed in any particular school of spirituality.

  • St. John of Ávila (1499?–1569) is one of the greatest Spanish mystics, who aroused St. Teresa's sincere admiration. He exercised great influence with his oral teaching and through his beautiful works Audi filia, Libro del Santísimo Sacramento, etc., and his marvelous Cartas (see Obras completas, ed. BAC, n. 89, 103).
  • St. Philip Neri (1515–1595), founder of the Oratory, Lettere, rime e detti memorabili (ed. Florencia 1922).
  • Lorenzo Scupoli (†1610) wrote the famous Il combattimento spirituale, much esteemed by St. Francis de Sales (numerous editions).
  • Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet (1627–1704), bishop of Meaux, Élévations sur les mystères, Méditations sur l'Évangile, Traité de la concupiscence, Instruction sur les états d'oraison (1617). He disputed about Fénelon and "pure love," and the Church gave the victory to Bossuet.
  • François de Salignac de la Mothe-Fénelon (1651–1715), archbishop of Cambrai, Explication des maximes de Saints sur la vie intérieure (1697), Sentiments de piété, Lettres spirituelles. In his controversy with the bishop of Meaux he came out defeated, submitting himself humbly to the dictamen of the Church.
  • St. Paul of the Cross (1694–1775), founder of the Passionists, Lettere (4 vols., ed. Roma 1924).
  • Frederick William Faber (1814–1863), All for Jesus, Bethlehem, The Blessed Sacrament, The Precious Blood, Growth in Holiness, etc. He is one of the most widely read authors of the past century.
  • St. John Henry Newman (1801–1890), besides his precious Sermons, has the Apologia (1864), Meditations and devotions (1895) and other spiritual writings.
  • St. Charles de Foucauld (1858–1916), Écrits spirituels (Paris 1923).
  • Auguste Saudreau (†1946) is one of the most commendable authors who has exercised the greatest influence in our times: Les degrés de la vie spirituelle (ed. Barcelona 1929), L'état mystique (Angers 1921), Manuel de spiritualité (1920), etc.
  • Jacques Maritain, Distinguer pour unir, ou Les degrés du savoir; De la vie d'oraison (Paris 1933).
